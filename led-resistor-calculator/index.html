Overview

Every LED will have a threshold level of current at which it can be safely handled, if the maximum current crosses the threshold value then we have to forget about that LED. So to avoid this damage we have to limit the current in the circuit by adding a suitable resistor to the circuit. Attention here buddy, the above-mentioned method is not for high current LEDs, which requires a more reliable switching current regulator.

This Calculator will help you to determine which resistor to be used to keep your LED out of damage.

Identifying LED Terminals

An LED has a positive (anode) lead and a negative (cathode) lead. The schematic symbol of the LED is similar to the diode (as shown above) except for two arrows pointing outwards. The anode (+) is marked with a triangle and the cathode (-) is marked with a line.

The longer lead of an LED is almost always the positive (anode) lead, while the shorter one is the negative (cathode). Also, if you look inside the LED, the smaller of the metal pieces is connected to the anode while the bigger one is connected to the cathode (see diagram above).

As you observe the above diagram, the cathode side will have a flat surface, and to the anode side it will be rounded surface.

By using these above three ways we can identify the terminals of an LED.
