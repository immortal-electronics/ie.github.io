Superconductivity

Superconductivity is a property of eliminating the electric resistance completely in some specific conducting materials when they are cooled below some specific temperatures. We call that specific temperature as Transition Temperature. This Transition temperature varies for different materials but one common point is all those values will be surely less than 20 K(273o C).

Superconductivity was first discovered in 1911 by H. Kamerlingh Onnes at the University of Leiden, Netherlands.

This superconductivity is not the concept of losing the electric resistance gradually point by point, but the conductor will lose its resistance to absolute zero at a sudden jerk when the conductor reaches its transition temperature.

We can’t use the superconductors along with strong magnets, because due to their strong magnetic fields will make the conductor to retain non-superconducting state even if it is maintained below the transition temperature.

The superconductors are mainly used for motors, generators, transformers, medical magnetic-imaging devices, magnetic energy-storage systems, computer parts, and very sensitive devices for measuring magnetic fields, voltages, or currents. Low power dissipation, high-speed operation, and high sensitivity are the key features of superconductors.

This superconductivity gives electric circuits an extrodrinary gain because as the resistance is eliminated completely there will be no power losses or inefficiencies in electric power systems due to stray resistances. Along with that, the electric motors can be made to run with a 100% efficiency with the help of superconductivity. The inherent wire resistance which will affect the ideal characteristics of capacitors and inductors can also be removed with the help of superconductivity.

The R&D is going on presently to bring the superconducting motors, conductors, etc., into the market, but these efforts are being restricted due to some practical problems like maintaining the ultra-cool temperatures constantly.
