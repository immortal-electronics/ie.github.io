Ohm’s Law

One of the most important and basic laws of Electronic and Electrical circuits is Ohm’s Law. In 1826, George Simon Ohm discovered this law. He found that the metallic conductors contain some substantially constant ratio of a potential difference between their two ends, this made him explain a law which states that the current in an electric circuit is directly proportional to applied voltage and inversely proportional to the resistance of the material, and he named this law as Ohm’s Law.

According to law,

Where, I= Current, V= Voltage, R= Resistance

These statements explain Ohm’s Law:

Current varies directly with applied voltage:

Let’s make the resistance constant in an electric circuit, then the current flow change in the circuit is directly proportional to the voltage applied. If we double the voltage, the current in the circuit also doubles.

Current varies inversely with resistance

We can also control the current in a circuit without changing voltage, for this just keep the applied voltage as constant and vary the circuit’s resistance. If the circuit’s resistance is high then it will restrict more amount of current so the value of current will be low, if the circuit’s resistance is low the current value rises up in the circuit. This show the current value is inversely proportional to the circuit’s resistance.

Ohm’s Law Triangle:

The Ohm’s law triangle is used to identify the equations of Ohm’s law easily.

If we want the equation for current then, from the triangle we can see that it is V/R (Observe the placement of Notations, the Horizontal line represents Divide-by and Verticle line represents Multiply).

If we want equation for Voltage then, from the triangle we can it is I.R

If we want equation for Resistance then, from the triangle we can it is V/I

Power:

The rate at which the energy is absorbed or produced within a circuit is called Power. The voltage will produce or deliver the power when it is connected to a load.
